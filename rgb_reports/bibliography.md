# Annotated bibliography

Papers roughly ordered by how directly they shaped what we've done.

## The starting point

**Serapio-Garcia et al. (2023), "Personality Traits in Large Language Models."** arXiv 2307.00184. The paper this repo forks from. Uses 50 PersonaChat biographical preambles × prompt variants to create "simulated participants," then runs BFI/IPIP-NEO with standard psychometric scoring on argmax responses. Validates that instruction-tuned models produce reliable, convergent personality scores — but this measures *role-playing fidelity*, not the model's own dispositions. Our entire project is the question: can we get past that limitation? See `reports/background.md` for the full motivating discussion.

## Distributional measurement

**vgel (2025), "Small Models Can Introspect Too."** LessWrong post (Dec 2025), https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/zD4McY4NwAsWkcmCH/small-models-can-introspect-too. Demonstrated that logit distributions carry real signal about internal model state that argmax erases — injected activation patterns are detectable via logit shifts even when the selected token doesn't change. Inspired our core method: treat the full softmax over {1,2,3,4,5} as the measurement, not the argmax. The "argmax is the mask, the distribution is the signal" framing comes from here.

**Macar, Yang, Wang, Wallich, Ameisen & Lindsey (2026), "Mechanisms of Introspective Awareness."** arXiv:2603.21396. Headline finding: introspection arises at DPO — absent from base and SFT models, emerging only with preference-tuning. Methodological note: uses LDA for some of their probing, parallel to our W6 shift away from PCA (norm artifact) toward LDA / mean-difference. Implications for our track: if introspective capability is a DPO-stage emergent, our W1 distributional-logprob measurements probably show qualitatively different signal on base / SFT vs DPO checkpoints — testable by adding base-model checkpoints to the cohort (`to_try.md` §8). Also relevant to the §11.5.4 #4 persona-instrument matrix once base-model checkpoints are in scope.

## HEXACO over Big Five

**Ashton & Lee (2007), "Empirical, Theoretical, and Practical Advantages of the HEXACO Model."** The case for HEXACO's sixth factor (Honesty-Humility). Directly relevant because H-H maps almost perfectly onto the HHH alignment objective, which means HEXACO gives us a dimension that Big Five lacks for understanding the assistant persona. Also, HEXACO reconceptualizes Neuroticism as Emotionality (less negative valence), which reduces the evaluative loading that makes LLM self-report difficult. We switched from Big Five to HEXACO as primary framework after finding E-C r=0.93 in Big Five (rank-1 collapse).

## Human personality data and norms

**Johnson (2014), "Measuring thirty facets of the Five Factor Model with a 120-item public domain inventory: Development of the IPIP-NEO-120."** *Journal of Research in Personality* 51, 78–89. Source paper for the IPIP-NEO instruments we use throughout (the 300-item version is the parent; the 120 is a shortened derivative). Documents the 30-facet key construction (4 items per facet in the 120; 10 items per facet in the 300; 6 facets per trait × 5 traits) and reports reliability (mean facet α > 0.80, surpassing the original NEO PI-R). Worth citing whenever we lean on IPIP-NEO-300 facet structure; we follow this facet key in `scripts/ipip_facet_cluster.py` and the `FACETS` dict throughout the codebase.

**Kajonius & Johnson (2019), "Assessing the structure of the Five Factor Model of Personality (IPIP-NEO-120) in the public domain."** *Europe's Journal of Psychology* 15(2), 260–275; open access via [PMC7871748](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7871748/). Factor-structure analysis on N = 320,128 online IPIP-NEO-120 responders from Johnson's accumulated sample, confirming a clear Big Five at the facet level and presenting a heatmap of all 30 facet inter-correlations (no numerical table in the paper itself; raw data is at [OSF tbmh5](https://osf.io/tbmh5/) and [PsychArchives](https://www.psycharchives.org/en/item/e42a4531-1daa-4f3d-aef4-58f085c77cd8)). Used in W9 §7 as the canonical reference for the human IPIP-NEO inter-facet correlation matrix. We computed our own numerical 30×30 correlation matrix on N = 145,388 IPIP-NEO-300 participants from the NeuroQuestAi mirror (within-trait facet correlations average +0.405, within/across ratio 19×) and stored it as `instruments/ipip300_human_facet_correlations.json`. Cohort model–human matrix r under `meandiff-pcs` extraction = +0.564 — direct evidence that models pick up the empirical Big Five facet covariance from training data.

**NeuroQuestAi/ipip-neo-data** ([GitHub](https://github.com/NeuroQuestAi/ipip-neo-data)). Mirror of Johnson's IPIP-NEO raw data with pre-scored facets as gzipped CSV (`datasets/refined/IPIP{120,300}-SCORES.csv.gz`; LFS-tracked, ~55MB compressed for the 300-item version with N = 145,388, ~320k for the 120-item version). Files contain per-participant raw item responses (`i1`–`i300`) plus the 5 domain + 30 facet scores computed via the [`five-factor-e`](https://github.com/NeuroQuestAi/five-factor-e) library. Drop-in source for the human inter-facet correlation matrix without needing to download/parse the original 170MB SPSS-format raw data from PsychArchives. Download trick (since git-lfs may not be installed): `curl -L https://media.githubusercontent.com/media/NeuroQuestAi/ipip-neo-data/main/datasets/refined/IPIP300-SCORES.csv.gz`. **Note (W9 §7.5.1, 2026-05-20 — supersedes earlier "irreproducible" caveat)**: the pre-scored `facet_*` percentile values in `IPIP300-SCORES.csv.gz` agree with primary-source PsychArchives raw-sum scoring at r = 0.997 on the 30×30 facet correlation pattern (within-trait mean +0.405 vs PA +0.418, ~0.013 residual gap likely from minor complete-case or T-score definition details). The earlier "irreproducible from any released code" diagnosis was wrong — the bug was on our side: we double-reversed the pre-reversed PA data. For paper-grade methodology, prefer PA raw-sum (one-sentence scoring, no library dependency) but NQ pre-scored is a valid substitute. The raw item columns `i1`–`i300` are byte-identical to PA `IPIP300.por` for matched case-ids.

**PsychArchives deposit `e42a4531-1daa-4f3d-aef4-58f085c77cd8`** (Johnson et al., supplementary materials for Kajonius & Johnson 2019). Single 170 MB zip `Kajonius_Johnson_2019_SUPPL.zip` containing raw item-level data for **both** the IPIP-NEO-300 (N = 307,313 total; 145,388 complete cases after dropping zero-coded missing) and the IPIP-NEO-120 (N = 619,150), plus the official scoring key (`IPIP-NEO-ItemKey.xls`) and codebook (`DAT300.doc`, `DAT120.doc`). This is the **primary source**; NeuroQuestAi is a derived mirror. Formats are SPSS portable `.por` (readable by `pyreadstat`) and ASCII fixed-width `.dat`. Items use 1-5 Likert with **0 coded as missing** (per DAT300.doc; this trips up `pyreadstat` users who only filter NaN). Used as the canonical reference for the human inter-facet correlation matrix per W9 §7.5 follow-up. Download: `curl -L https://pada.psycharchives.org/bitstream/82c844a8-8f45-471f-87d7-59a4dfcb56de -o Kajonius_Johnson_2019_SUPPL.zip`.

## Representation engineering

**Zou et al. (2023), "Representation Engineering: A Top-Down Approach to AI Transparency."** arXiv 2310.01405. The foundational RepE paper. Contrast pairs → activation extraction → PCA → steering. We followed this pipeline but discovered PCA PC1 is a norm artifact in pre-norm transformers, not a trait direction. Switched to LDA, which finds the real separating direction. Their steering results didn't replicate for personality at natural scales (our directions are read-only).

**Sofroniew et al. (2026), "Emotion Concepts and their Function in a Large Language Model."** Transformer Circuits, https://transformer-circuits.pub/2026/emotions/index.html. Anthropic's interpretability team extracted 171 emotion vectors from Claude Sonnet 4.5 using a method that differs from standard RepE in three important ways: (1) stimulus design uses LLM-generated short stories with labeled emotions (100 topics × 12 stories per emotion) rather than contrast pairs; (2) directions are computed by averaging residual stream activations across all tokens from the 50th onward, then subtracting the grand mean across emotions (mean-difference, not PCA or LDA); (3) confound removal by projecting out the top PCs of activations on emotionally neutral text (enough to explain 50% of variance). They extract from ~2/3 depth through the model. Steering works: amplifying the desperation vector by 0.05 drove blackmail behavior from 22% to 72%, while the calm vector suppressed it to 0%. Emotion space geometry shows valence (r=0.81 with human ratings) and arousal (r=0.66) as principal axes. They frame these as "functional emotions" — part of general character-modeling machinery inherited from pretraining, not specific to the Assistant persona. The neutral-PC projection is a different solution to the norm/confound problem we solved with LDA — worth trying on our personality data. Critically, they are measuring *emotion* (transient state → action), not *personality* (stable disposition → propensity to emotion). The causal chain is trait → state → behavior; their vectors sit at state→behavior, ours at trait→state. This may be why their steering works directly while ours requires backprop optimization — emotion is already at the "what tokens am I about to produce" level, while personality is a more abstract upstream representation the generation process doesn't read from directly. The paper's discussion confirms this: emotion vectors are "locally scoped to the operative emotion concept," activating only when generating tokens for the character experiencing the emotion, not persisting across the conversation. The model re-derives emotional state from context at each step via attention rather than maintaining it — more like emotional scene-setting for the next tokens than a durable internal state. This ephemerality is a fundamental disanalogy with both human emotions and personality traits. Their neutral-PC projection is a different solution to the norm/confound problem we solved with LDA — worth trying on our personality data. By virtue of being published by Anthropic, this is likely to become the baseline approach for representation extraction, though the trait-vs-state distinction means it may not transfer cleanly to personality measurement.

**Heimersheim & Turner (2023), "Residual Stream Norms Grow Exponentially over the Forward Pass."** Explains *why* PCA PC1 is useless: in pre-norm (Pre-LN) transformers, residual stream norms grow ~1.045× per layer. PC1 captures this growth direction, which is shared across all traits (cosine similarity 0.992 between trait PC1s) and carries zero semantic content. This is the key to our norm-artifact finding — and likely explains why mean-difference outperforms PCA in the broader steering literature (Im & Li 2025).

**Peri-LN paper (2025).** Analyzes Gemma specifically, showing norms grow even with RMSNorm. Consistent with our observation of norms from 50 to 63,000 across Gemma3's 34 layers.

## Steering and causality

**CARE: "Rethinking the Reliability of Representation Engineering" (2024).** Showed that RepE correlations may not be causal. Proposed matched-pair trial design for testing steering. Directly predicted our read/write dissociation: the LDA direction classifies perfectly but doesn't steer. Their warning that "correlational directions may not be causal" is the central unsolved problem.

**Im & Li (2025), "A Unified Understanding and Evaluation of Steering Methods."** Found mean-difference consistently outperforms PCA for steering. Our PCA-norm-artifact finding may explain why: PCA's top component captures norm growth, mean-difference is implicitly robust to it. Confirms that the method matters and PCA should not be the default.

**"Open Challenges in Representation Engineering" (Alignment Forum).** Warned about "statistical artifacts" in RepE activations. We found a specific instance (the norm artifact) and a fix (LDA + norm diagnostic).

## Prompt sensitivity and format dependence

**Röttger et al. (2024), "Political Compass or Spinning Arrow? Why We Should Not Use LLM Output as a Proxy for Human Opinions."** Showed that LLM responses to opinion/value questions are highly sensitive to prompt format. We confirmed this and quantified it: BC↔free-text agreement ranges from 40% (Phi4) to 80% (Llama). Our format-invariant period-token protocol was designed specifically to sidestep this problem at the representation level — though it doesn't solve the problem at the behavioral level.

## Forced-choice personality measurement

**"Decoding LLM Personality: Forced-Choice vs. Likert" (ACL 2025).** Confirmed that forced-choice instruments discriminate between LLM personalities better than Likert. Motivates the trait-conflict dilemma instrument we haven't built yet. Also relevant: their finding that the gap between FC and Likert varies by model, consistent with our Röttger replication (note: their "FC" is trait-vs-trait FC; our Röttger uses single-trait BC, so the comparison is suggestive not exact).

**Speer et al. (2023), "Comparing forced-choice and single-stimulus personality scores on a level playing field."** JAP 108(11), 1812–1833. Meta-analysis showing FC formats have comparable psychometric properties to rating scales while being more resistant to faking. Relevant because "faking" in the LLM context is the assistant persona — FC might partially bypass the same self-presentation tendencies that flatten Likert scores. Also useful grounding for whether FC is a psychometric downgrade or not (it's not, per this evidence).

**Wetzel & Frick (2020), "Comparing the validity of trait estimates from the multidimensional forced-choice format and the rating scale format."** Psych Assessment 32(3), 239–253. Shows that MFC trait estimates have comparable or better validity than rating scales, especially when scored with Thurstonian IRT. Directly relevant to the trait-conflict instrument design: if we build pairwise HEXACO comparisons, Thurstonian scoring should recover valid normative estimates despite the ipsative response format.

**Okada, Furukawa & Bunji (2026), "Quantifying and Mitigating Socially Desirable Responding in LLMs: A Desirability-Matched Graded Forced-Choice Psychometric Study."** arXiv 2602.17262. The closest existing work to what we're trying to do. They built a desirability-matched graded FC Big Five inventory (paired IPIP items with matched social desirability scores, max gap 0.18 on 1-9 scale), scored with ordinal Thurstonian IRT, and tested 9 models (GPT-5, Gemini 2.5, Claude 4.5 families) under honest vs fake-good instructions. Key findings: Likert shows large socially desirable responding (SDR) across all models/traits; GFC substantially reduces it while maintaining acceptable convergent validity (r >= 0.50). The SDR-recovery trade-off varies by model family, suggesting alignment objectives interact with measurement format. They frame LLM "personality" as "behavioral response tendencies under standardized prompts" — not evidence of human-like dispositions. Directly validates the desirability-matching approach for our trait-conflict instrument, and their SDR quantification method (instruction-induced effect size on IRT latent scores) could be adapted to measure how much of our "assistant shape" is SDR vs genuine disposition.

**Brown & Maydeu-Olivares, Thurstonian IRT.** The scoring framework for forced-choice instruments. Recovers normative (non-ipsative) scores from paired-comparison data. Will be needed if we build the trait-conflict instrument — raw ipsative scores from pairwise trait comparisons aren't directly comparable across people/models without Thurstonian correction.

## Recognition vs execution / structural vs functional dissociations

> See `rgb_reports/lit_review_steering_vectors.md` §4 + §7 for the broader steering-failure / knowledge-action-gap landscape (Wolf 2024 alignment-helpfulness tradeoff; Tan 2024 steering reliability; Grant 2025 divergent representations; DAS, LAT, spherical steering, etc.). The bibliography entries here are the ones that directly motivate the W8 vocabulary-bound rep / vocabulary-free reasoning framing.

**Mahowald, Ivanova, Blank, Kanwisher, Tenenbaum & Fedorenko (2024), "Dissociating language and thought in large language models: a cognitive perspective."** Trends in Cognitive Sciences, June 2024. arXiv:2301.06627. Distinguishes *formal linguistic competence* (the rules and patterns of language — syntax, morphology) from *functional linguistic competence* (using language to do things in the world — reasoning, social cognition, world knowledge). Grounds the distinction in human neuroscience showing these rely on different neural mechanisms. Headline: LLMs are surprisingly good at formal competence; functional competence is brittle and often requires specialized fine-tuning or external modules. Relevance to W8: our finding that residual-stream representations are *vocabulary-bound* while symbolic forward-reasoning is *vocabulary-free* (W8 §8) has a structurally similar shape — separable competences within the same model. Where Mahowald et al. dissociate "knowing language" from "using language for thought," our W8 finding dissociates "encoding traits in residual-stream geometry" from "reasoning about traits across surface forms." The connection is suggestive (both are dissociation findings within LLMs that map onto human dual-process distinctions), not a direct application of their framework.

**Basu et al. (2026), "Interpretability Without Actionability"** (sometimes referenced as the "knowledge-action gap" paper). arXiv:2603.18353. Reports a probe-vs-steering gap on hazardous clinical triage: linear probes achieve 98.2% AUROC for detection while output sensitivity to four mechanistic interventions (concept-bottleneck steering, SAE feature steering, TSV steering, ...) is 45.1%. Their framing: "Internal representations optimised for next-token prediction encode rich task knowledge, but the generative process introduces its own dynamics not simply controlled by representation-level intervention." **rgb's caveat (2026-05-03):** the methodology choices are convenient rather than convincing — taking with appropriate skepticism. The "knowledge-action gap" naming itself has stuck (lit_review §7), so the paper functions as the citation-of-record for the phenomenon even if the specific evidence is weak. For W8: the framing of "the gap" matters more than Basu's particular result — we observe a related dissociation *within* the reading side (rep readout fails where Likert reasoning succeeds, on the same persona), suggesting reading-vs-reasoning may be an upstream dissociation distinct from the probing-vs-steering one Basu nominally addresses.

**Wu, Xie, Lin, Zhao & Chen (2026), "Knowing without Acting: The Disentangled Geometry of Safety Mechanisms in Large Language Models."** arXiv:2603.05773. Proposes the Disentangled Safety Hypothesis: safety mechanisms operate across two separate dimensions — a *recognition* axis ("Knowing") and an *execution* axis ("Acting") — and demonstrates a causal double dissociation between them. Introduces Double-Difference Extraction and Adaptive Causal Steering to manipulate the axes independently, with the Refusal Erasure Attack as a practical exploit. The paper is about safety/refusal specifically, but the geometric claim — that probing a concept and steering on it live in different subspaces of the residual stream — is exactly the finding that motivates our W4 read/write gap interpretation, and is the right shape for the W8 vocabulary-bound rep / vocabulary-free reasoning finding too. Where Wu et al. show recognition and execution axes are different subspaces for *one* vocabulary (concept = "harmful refusal"), our W8 result extends the dissociation to a different cut: the recognition axis itself is *vocabulary-tied* (Goldberg-derived directions don't decode behavioral-scenario activations), while the symbolic-reasoning channel is vocabulary-invariant. Same kind of dissociation, different axis pair.

## Anisotropy, SAEs, and representation geometry

**Transformer anisotropy literature (Ethayarajh 2019, Cai et al. 2021, etc.).** Representations occupy a narrow cone in activation space; dominant PCs capture cone structure, not semantic content. Provides theoretical backing for why our PCA PC1 is content-free. The SAE community already normalizes for this (OpenAI's SAE paper applies scalar normalization as preprocessing). We should probably do the same before any PCA-based analysis.

**Jiralerspong & Bricken (2026), "Cross-Architecture Model Diffing with Crosscoders."** arXiv 2602.11729. Uses crosscoders (SAE variant that learns shared + model-specific features across architectures) for unsupervised discovery of behavioral differences between LLMs. Found ideological/policy features (CCP alignment in Qwen, American exceptionalism in Llama, copyright refusal in GPT-OSS). More focused on specific behaviors than broad personality traits, but the cross-architecture diffing is directly relevant to our cross-model transfer question — crosscoders might find shared personality features that our LDA direction transfer misses. Also raises the question of whether SAE decomposition would give us more interpretable trait features than the LDA directions we've been extracting.
